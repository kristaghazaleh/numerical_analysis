Introduction

Numerical analysis studies the gap between the exact mathematical object and the quantity that a real computation can actually produce. The subject is not just about finding formulas for algorithms. It is about turning a mathematical problem into a computable one, then measuring how truncation, perturbation, and roundoff affect the output. Every method on this page should be read through the same chain: what is the exact target, what is the approximation, what quantity measures the discrepancy, what theorem controls that discrepancy, and what happens after one more computational step.

In this course, notation matters. The exact solution of a nonlinear equation is usually written as \(r\), the approximation after \(n\) steps is \(x_n\), and the error is \(e_n = x_n-r\). For linear systems, the exact solution of \(Ax=b\) is often \(x\), an approximate solution is \(\tilde x\), the error vector is \(e=x-\tilde x\), and the residual is \(r=b-A\tilde x\). For interpolation, \(f\) is the exact function and \(p_n\) is the interpolating polynomial of degree at most \(n\). These symbols are not cosmetic. Nearly every theorem is a precise statement about how one of these quantities is bounded by another.

Much of the course can be divided into three layers. First comes the exact theorem: existence, uniqueness, convergence, stability, conditioning, or error. Next comes a practical bound, often obtained from the Mean Value Theorem, Taylor's theorem with remainder, Rolle's theorem, or a matrix norm inequality. Finally comes the computation: choose a method, run it, and explain why the resulting numbers do or do not reflect the exact target accurately.

Foundations

Sequences, limits, and error variables

A sequence in \(\mathbb R\) is a function from the positive integers into \(\mathbb R\), written as \((x_n)=(x_1,x_2,x_3,\dots)\). A sequence converges to \(x\) if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists an integer \(N\) such that \(|x_n-x|<\varepsilon\) whenever \(n>N\). This epsilon-definition is the real foundation of every convergence theorem in the course. Any time a proof says a sequence converges, this is the statement it ultimately means.

If \(x\) is the exact target and \(x_n\) is the nth approximation, then the error is \(e_n=x_n-x\). For nonlinear equations the exact target is usually a root \(r\), so \(e_n=x_n-r\). For fixed-point iteration one often studies \(x_{n+1}=F(x_n)\) and rewrites it as \[ e_{n+1}=F(x_n)-F(r), \qquad \text{where } r=F(r). \] That is the move that brings the Mean Value Theorem into the analysis.

Rates of convergence

A sequence converging to \(x\) has linear convergence if there exists a constant \(C\) with \(0<C<1\) such that for all large \(n\), \[ |x_{n+1}-x|\le C|x_n-x|. \] It has superlinear convergence if there are numbers \(\omega_n\to 0\) such that \[ |x_{n+1}-x|\le \omega_n |x_n-x|. \] It has quadratic convergence if there exists \(C>0\) with \[ |x_{n+1}-x|\le C|x_n-x|^2 \] for all large \(n\). More generally, convergence has order \(\alpha>1\) if the next error behaves asymptotically like a constant times the current error raised to the power \(\alpha\).

These definitions tell you what successive iterations mean. Linear convergence means the error loses roughly a fixed fraction each step. Quadratic convergence means that once the iterates are close enough, the error is squared, so very rapid improvement follows. Superlinear convergence lies between those two behaviors. In practice, many questions about method comparison are really questions about which of these recurrence patterns the errors satisfy.

Big-Oh notation

Writing \(x_n = O(\omega_n)\) means that there exist constants \(C>0\) and \(N\) such that \(|x_n|\le C|\omega_n|\) whenever \(n>N\). This is an asymptotic boundedness statement. It is used constantly after Taylor expansion and in local error analysis. If a proof ends with a remainder term like \(O(h^2)\), it means the remainder is bounded in size by a constant times \(|h|^2\) when \(h\) is small.

Calculus facts used throughout the course

The Mean Value Theorem says that if \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\), then there exists \(\xi\in(a,b)\) such that \[ f'(\xi)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \] This theorem turns differences of function values into derivatives. It appears in fixed-point uniqueness, local conditioning, and many comparison arguments. If a question involves two nearby function values and asks for a bound, the Mean Value Theorem is often the first thing to try.

Rolle's theorem says that if \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\), and \(f(a)=f(b)\), then there exists \(\xi\in(a,b)\) with \(f'(\xi)=0\). In interpolation, repeated use of Rolle's theorem gives the uniqueness theorem and the interpolation remainder formula.

Taylor's theorem with remainder says that if \(f\in C^{n+1}\) near \(c\), then \[ f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(c)}{k!}(x-c)^k + \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!}(x-c)^{n+1} \] for some \(\xi\) between \(c\) and \(x\). This is the engine behind Newton error analysis, interpolation error formulas, and local stability approximations. The remainder term is where the actual bound comes from.

How to use the foundational theorems

If a problem asks for a rate, define an error variable immediately and try to relate \(e_{n+1}\) to \(e_n\). If it asks why a fixed point is unique, subtract the two fixed-point equations and use the Mean Value Theorem. If it asks why too many zeros force a polynomial to vanish, think about repeated differentiation and Rolle's theorem. If it asks for a local approximation or the source of a remainder term, Taylor's theorem is usually the correct starting point.

Floating-Point Arithmetic, Error, and Stability

Absolute error, relative error, and perturbation language

If an exact number \(x\) is approximated by \(\tilde x\), then the absolute error is \(|x-\tilde x|\), and the relative error is \(|x-\tilde x|/|x|\) when \(x\neq 0\). Absolute error measures raw distance between exact and approximate values. Relative error measures how large that distance is compared to the scale of the exact value. In most scientific settings relative error is the more meaningful quantity because losing \(10^{-4}\) means something very different near \(10^6\) than it does near \(10^{-6}\).

A perturbation means a small change in the input data. Conditioning studies how a problem reacts to perturbations. Stability studies how an algorithm reacts to small errors introduced during the computation itself. This distinction is fundamental. A problem can be well-conditioned but attacked by an unstable algorithm, or badly conditioned but handled by a stable algorithm.

Floating-point numbers and machine precision

Floating-point arithmetic stores only finitely many real numbers exactly. A nonzero floating-point number is represented in normalized scientific notation with sign, exponent, and mantissa. Most real numbers therefore must be replaced by nearby machine numbers. Chopping discards digits beyond the machine's storage limit. Rounding replaces a number by the nearest representable machine number. Rounding usually has the smaller worst-case error.

The machine epsilon is the size of the smallest relative perturbation that the arithmetic necessarily introduces at the scale of 1. In simplified error models, one frequently writes \[ fl(x)=x(1+\delta), \qquad |\delta|\le \varepsilon_{mach}, \] meaning that one floating-point operation replaces the exact value by a nearby relative perturbation.

Condition number of a scalar function

If \(f:(a,b)\to \mathbb R\) is differentiable and \(f(x)\neq 0\), the local condition number at \(x\) is \[ \kappa_f(x)=\left|\frac{x f'(x)}{f(x)}\right|. \] This comes from Taylor expansion. If the input is perturbed from \(x\) to \(x+h\), then \[ f(x+h)-f(x)=f'(x)h+O(h^2). \] After dividing by \(f(x)\), the relative output change is approximately \(\kappa_f(x)\) times the relative input change. So the derivative-based formula is not arbitrary; it is the linearization of relative sensitivity.

Instability and catastrophic cancellation

A numerical process is called unstable when small errors created at one stage are magnified in later stages and significantly damage the final output. The classic danger is catastrophic cancellation, which occurs when subtracting nearly equal quantities. If leading digits cancel, the remaining digits may consist mostly of previous rounding error instead of actual information from the exact numbers.

Standard examples include the smaller root in the quadratic formula, expressions such as \(\sqrt{x+1}-\sqrt{x}\) for large \(x\), and \((\cos x-1)/x^2\) near zero. The usual cure is not to compute with more hope, but to rewrite the expression in an algebraically equivalent but numerically safer form. Rationalizing, using a conjugate, or switching to a Taylor approximation are all stability repairs.

How to use these ideas in practice

When a problem asks about accuracy, first decide whether it is really about the mathematical problem or the computational formula. If it gives a function and asks about sensitivity to perturbing the input, compute or estimate a condition number. If it gives an explicit arithmetic expression and asks whether the computation is safe, look for subtraction of nearly equal numbers, division by very small numbers, or repeated amplification of previously rounded quantities. If a formula is unstable, produce a more stable equivalent expression and explain why it avoids the dangerous cancellation.

Nonlinear Equations and Rootfinding

General notation and setup

The standard nonlinear problem is to solve \(f(r)=0\) for an unknown root \(r\). An iterative method generates approximations \(x_0,x_1,x_2,\dots\). The central questions are whether \(x_n\to r\), what hypotheses make that happen, and what recurrence the errors \(e_n=x_n-r\) satisfy. When the root is simple, meaning \(f(r)=0\) and \(f'(r)\neq 0\), Newton and secant have their standard local convergence theorems. Multiple roots behave differently and typically reduce the order of convergence.

Bisection method

The bisection method starts with a continuous function on \([a_0,b_0]\) such that \(f(a_0)f(b_0)<0\). By the Intermediate Value Theorem there exists a root \(r\in(a_0,b_0)\). Let \[ c_n=\frac{a_n+b_n}{2}. \] One chooses the half-interval where the sign change persists and repeats.

Theorem-level facts for bisection are explicit and global. The interval width halves each step: \[ b_n-a_n=2^{-n}(b_0-a_0), \] and because the root stays in the interval, \[ |r-c_n|\le \frac{b_n-a_n}{2}=2^{-(n+1)}(b_0-a_0). \] This is why the method is linearly convergent and robust. The important point is that bisection does not use local smoothness to jump toward a root. It shrinks uncertainty geometrically.

In practice, if the problem asks how many bisection steps are needed for error less than \(\tau\), solve \[ 2^{-(n+1)}(b_0-a_0)\le \tau \] for \(n\). If the problem instead asks for a guaranteed interval containing the root after \(n\) steps, report the new interval width and midpoint bound.

Newton's method

Newton's iteration is \[ x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}. \] Geometrically, \(x_{n+1}\) is where the tangent line at \((x_n,f(x_n))\) crosses the x-axis. The formula itself is simple, but the reason it is fast comes from Taylor expansion near a simple root.

The local Newton theorem from the notes says: if \(f\in C^2(\mathbb R)\), \(r\) is a simple root, and the iterates remain in a sufficiently small interval around \(r\), then there exists \(C>0\) such that \[ |x_{n+1}-r|\le C|x_n-r|^2. \] This is the precise quadratic convergence statement. The hypotheses matter. One needs smoothness, a simple root, and a starting point close enough that the local tangent-line picture is accurate.

To apply Newton's theorem on a problem, first check the hypotheses: is \(f\in C^2\) on the interval of interest, is the desired root simple, and does the initial guess seem to lie near that root? Then define \(e_n=x_n-r\), Taylor expand \(f(x_n)\) around \(r\), and identify the leading error behavior. If the question asks why Newton can fail, common reasons are \(f'(x_n)=0\), a poor initial guess, a multiple root, or an iterate leaving the local basin of attraction.

Secant method

The secant method replaces the derivative in Newton's step with a finite-difference slope: \[ x_{n+1}=x_n-f(x_n)\frac{x_n-x_{n-1}}{f(x_n)-f(x_{n-1})}. \] It uses two previous iterates and requires no exact derivative. The asymptotic error theorem in the notes says that if \(f\in C^3(\mathbb R)\), \(r\) is a simple root, and \(x_n\to r\), then the error satisfies an asymptotic relation that leads to convergence order \[ \alpha=\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}. \] So secant is superlinear: faster than linear convergence, slower than Newton's quadratic convergence.

In practice, secant is useful when derivatives are expensive or unavailable. On a theoretical problem, the main point is usually to compare its error order to Newton and bisection, or to explain how approximating the derivative changes the convergence mechanism.

Fixed-point iteration

A fixed point of \(F\) is a number \(s\) such that \(F(s)=s\). To solve \(f(r)=0\), one often rearranges the equation into \(x=F(x)\) and iterates \[ x_{n+1}=F(x_n). \] The method is therefore only as good as the chosen rearrangement. Different rearrangements of the same equation can produce completely different convergence behavior.

The notes include three important theorem-level facts. First, if \(F:(0,1)\to(0,1)\) is differentiable and \(|F'(x)|<1\) for all \(x\in(0,1)\), then there is at most one fixed point; this is a Mean Value Theorem argument. Second, if \(F:[a,b]\to[a,b]\) is continuous, then it has at least one fixed point; this follows from the Intermediate Value Theorem applied to \(g(x)=F(x)-x\). Third, the Contraction Mapping Theorem says that if \(F:[a,b]\to[a,b]\) and \(|F'(x)|\le \omega<1\) on \([a,b]\), then there exists a unique fixed point \(s\in[a,b]\), every iteration starting in \([a,b]\) converges to \(s\), and \[ |s-x_n|\le \frac{\omega^n}{1-\omega}|x_1-x_0|. \]

To use fixed-point theory on a problem, check three things in order. First, does \(F\) map the interval into itself? Second, is \(F\) continuous or differentiable on that interval? Third, is \(|F'(x)|\le \omega<1\) there? If all three hold, then the theorem gives existence, uniqueness, convergence, and an error bound. If \(|F'(s)|>1\), nearby errors are pushed away. If \(F'(s)<0\), oscillation is common. If \(F'(s)=0\) and \(F''(s)\neq 0\), the notes show the method can become quadratically convergent.

Roots of polynomials and Horner's method

Horner's method rewrites \[ p(x)=a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1x+a_0 \] in nested form, \[ p(x)=(((a_nx+a_{n-1})x+a_{n-2})x+\cdots)x+a_0. \] This lowers the arithmetic cost of evaluation and is numerically preferable to naive computation of each power. The same recurrence can also be adapted to express a polynomial in powers of \((x-c)\), which is useful when a problem asks for a shifted form or synthetic-division style coefficient extraction.

Linear Systems, Elimination, Factorizations, and Norms

Basic definitions

An elementary row operation is one of the following: swap two rows, multiply a row by a nonzero scalar, or add a scalar multiple of one row to another. An elementary matrix is the matrix obtained by performing one elementary row operation on the identity matrix. If \(E\) is elementary, then left multiplication by \(E\) performs that row operation on a compatible matrix. This is the algebraic reason elimination can be written as a product of elementary matrices.

A matrix \(L\) is lower triangular if entries above the diagonal are zero. A matrix \(U\) is upper triangular if entries below the diagonal are zero. Triangular systems are computationally easy because they are solved by forward or backward substitution.

An LU factorization is a representation \(A=LU\), with \(L\) lower triangular and \(U\) upper triangular. If row interchanges are needed, the correct form is \(PA=LU\), where \(P\) is a permutation matrix. Determinants, invertibility, and efficient repeated solving are all tied to these factorizations.

Invertibility equivalences

One of the central theorems of the linear systems unit says that for a square matrix \(A\), the following are equivalent: \(A^{-1}\) exists, \(\det(A)\neq 0\), the system \(Ax=b\) has a unique solution for every \(b\), the homogeneous system \(Ax=0\) has only the trivial solution, and \(A\) can be written as a product of elementary matrices. This theorem is the reason so many questions about solvability, determinants, elimination, and inverse matrices are really the same question in different language.

Gaussian elimination and pivoting

Gaussian elimination builds an upper triangular system by zeroing entries below each pivot. In exact arithmetic this is an algebraically correct transformation of the system. In floating-point arithmetic the numerical danger comes from small pivots. Dividing by a tiny pivot produces large multipliers, and those large multipliers can amplify roundoff that was created earlier.

Partial pivoting chooses the largest available entry in the current column as the pivot. Scaled partial pivoting compares pivot candidates after normalizing by row scale. These strategies do not change the exact mathematical solution of the system, but they can drastically improve numerical behavior by keeping multipliers moderate.

On practical problems, elimination questions often have two layers: the symbolic elimination itself and the numerical quality of the elimination. If the data are exact, the main goal is to track row operations correctly. If the problem mentions finite precision, then the size of the pivots and multipliers matters just as much as the formal elimination steps.

Norms and subordinate matrix norms

A norm on a vector space is a function \(\|\cdot\|\) satisfying positivity, homogeneity, and the triangle inequality. On \(\mathbb R^n\), the common examples are \[ \|x\|_1=\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|, \qquad \|x\|_2=\left(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2\right)^{1/2}, \qquad \|x\|_\infty=\max_i |x_i|. \]

Given a vector norm, the associated subordinate matrix norm is \[ \|A\|=\max_{\|u\|=1}\|Au\|. \] The notes prove this is a true matrix norm. The consequences you actually use are \[ \|Ax\|\le \|A\|\,\|x\|, \qquad \|AB\|\le \|A\|\,\|B\|, \qquad \|I\|=1. \] These are the inequalities that make perturbation and residual bounds work.

The matrix \(2\)-norm is \[ \|A\|_2=\max_{\|x\|_2=1}\|Ax\|_2. \] In many theoretical problems it is defined abstractly, while in applications one may connect it to singular values.

Residual, error, and condition number

If \(\tilde x\) approximates the exact solution \(x\) of \(Ax=b\), then the error vector is \[ e=x-\tilde x, \] and the residual is \[ r=b-A\tilde x. \] These are linked by \[ r=Ae. \] So a small residual does not automatically imply a small error unless \(A^{-1}\) is well-behaved.

The condition number of an invertible matrix in a chosen subordinate norm is \[ \kappa(A)=\|A\|\,\|A^{-1}\|. \] The theorem in the notes gives the residual-error comparison \[ \frac{1}{\kappa(A)}\frac{\|r\|}{\|b\|} \le \frac{\|e\|}{\|x\|} \le \kappa(A)\frac{\|r\|}{\|b\|}. \] This is one of the most important inequalities in the course. It explains why residual and error are not the same object, and why big condition numbers are dangerous.

Iterative refinement

Iterative refinement starts from an approximate solution \(\tilde x\), computes the residual \(r=b-A\tilde x\), solves \[ Ay=r, \] and updates \(\tilde x\leftarrow \tilde x+y\). The logic is simple: because \(r=Ae\), solving \(Ay=r\) tries to approximate the remaining error directly. Refinement is therefore a residual-correction method. It works best when the residual can be computed accurately and the linear system solver is reasonably stable.

Polynomial Interpolation, Divided Differences, and Hermite Data

The interpolation problem and uniqueness

Given distinct nodes \(x_0,x_1,\dots,x_n\) and data values \(y_0,y_1,\dots,y_n\), the interpolation problem asks for a polynomial \(p\) of degree at most \(n\) such that \[ p(x_j)=y_j, \qquad j=0,1,\dots,n. \] The notes prove the basic theorem: if \(x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_n\), then for any real values \(y_0,\dots,y_n\) there exists a unique polynomial of degree at most \(n\) satisfying these conditions.

The uniqueness proof is one of the standard course arguments. If \(p\) and \(q\) both interpolate the same data, then \(p-q\) is a polynomial of degree at most \(n\) with \(n+1\) distinct zeros. A nonzero polynomial of degree at most \(n\) cannot have more than \(n\) roots, so \(p-q\equiv 0\). This is the exact reason distinct nodes matter.

Lagrange form

The Lagrange basis polynomials are \[ \ell_k(x)=\prod_{j\ne k}\frac{x-x_j}{x_k-x_j}, \] and they satisfy \(\ell_k(x_j)=\delta_{kj}\), where \(\delta_{kj}\) is the Kronecker delta. The interpolating polynomial is therefore \[ p(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n y_k\,\ell_k(x). \] This form is conceptually clean because each basis function isolates one data value. It is usually the best form for proving existence and understanding the geometry of interpolation, though not always the best for computation.

Newton form and divided differences

The Newton form is \[ p_n(x)=c_0+c_1(x-x_0)+c_2(x-x_0)(x-x_1)+\cdots+c_n\prod_{j=0}^{n-1}(x-x_j). \] The coefficients \(c_k\) are the divided differences. They are defined recursively by \[ f[x_0]=f(x_0), \qquad f[x_0,x_1]=\frac{f(x_1)-f(x_0)}{x_1-x_0}, \] and more generally \[ f[x_0,\dots,x_n]=\frac{f[x_1,\dots,x_n]-f[x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}]}{x_n-x_0}. \] The highest-order divided difference over the listed nodes is exactly the leading Newton coefficient.

Newton form is especially useful when nodes are added one at a time or when a problem asks you to build a divided-difference table. The table is not busywork; it is a systematic way to recover the coefficients of the Newton basis.

Interpolation remainder theorem

The key error theorem from the notes says: if \(f\in C^{n+1}(\mathbb R)\) and \(p\) is the interpolating polynomial of degree at most \(n\) at nodes \(x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_n\), then for each \(x\in[x_0,x_n]\) there exists \(\omega\in(x_0,x_n)\) such that \[ f(x)-p(x)=\frac{f^{(n+1)}(\omega)}{(n+1)!}\prod_{j=0}^n (x-x_j). \] This formula is the main error tool for interpolation. It splits the error into a smoothness term, namely \(f^{(n+1)}\), and a node-geometry term, namely the product \(\prod (x-x_j)\).

To apply the theorem on an actual problem, use the same workflow every time. First write the remainder formula. Second take absolute values, because the problem usually asks for an upper bound on the size of the error. Third bound \(|f^{(n+1)}(\omega)|\) on the relevant interval. Fourth bound the node product \(\left|\prod (x-x_j)\right|\). Fifth combine the bounds and solve the resulting inequality for whichever parameter the problem asks for, typically \(n\), \(h\), or the maximum error itself.

This section is also where preliminary differential-equation information becomes useful. If the function is not given in a simple closed form but instead satisfies a differential equation, that equation is often there to help bound derivatives. For example, if \(f''(x)=x f(x)\), then the interpolation remainder for a linear interpolant involves \(f''\), and one can bound it using known information about \(x\) and \(f(x)\). For a quadratic interpolant the remainder involves \(f'''\), so one differentiates the differential equation to get \[ f'''(x)=f(x)+x f'(x). \] That is not a separate trick. It is exactly how the ODE feeds the derivative term in the interpolation remainder.

Another recurring situation is when the theorem gives a factorial inequality such as \[ \frac{1}{(n+1)!} < 10^{-7}. \] In that case one does not solve for \(n\) algebraically. One checks factorials until the bound becomes small enough. The point of the problem is usually the theorem and the bound construction, not the factorial arithmetic itself.

Hermite interpolation

Hermite interpolation prescribes both function values and derivative values. Given nodes \(x_0<\cdots<x_n\), values \(y_j\), and derivative values \(y'_j\), one seeks a polynomial \(p\) such that \[ p(x_j)=y_j, \qquad p'(x_j)=y'_j, \qquad j=0,1,\dots,n. \] The theorem in the notes says there exists a unique polynomial of degree at most \(2n+1\) satisfying these conditions.

In divided-difference language, repeated nodes encode derivative information. The fundamental definition is \[ f[x_0,x_0]=f'(x_0). \] This is the bridge from ordinary interpolation tables to Hermite tables. Once repeated nodes are introduced correctly, the Newton-form machinery still applies.

Problem Patterns

Typical theorem-to-problem matches

If the problem gives a continuous function with opposite signs at the endpoints, it is probably asking for the Intermediate Value Theorem and a bisection setup. If it asks whether a rearranged iteration converges, it is probably asking whether \(F\) maps an interval into itself and whether \(|F'|\le \omega<1\). If it asks for a local rate near a root, define the error and think Taylor expansion. If it gives interpolation data and asks for a bound, it is almost certainly asking for the interpolation remainder theorem. If it gives both values and derivatives at nodes, the right object is Hermite interpolation.

What kind of error is the problem really asking about?

Always identify the exact object being bounded. In rootfinding, the relevant quantity is usually \(|x_n-r|\). In linear systems it may be either \(\|e\|\) or \(\|r\|\), and those are not interchangeable without a condition-number argument. In floating-point problems the issue may be relative error in the data or instability of the arithmetic formula. In interpolation it is usually \(|f(x)-p_n(x)|\). A large amount of confusion disappears once the right error quantity has been named explicitly.

How to Analyze Successive Iterations

The error-dynamics viewpoint

Questions about successive iterations are really asking how one update transforms the previous error. Let \(r\) be the exact target. Define \(e_n=x_n-r\). Then rewrite the iteration so the next step is expressed in terms of the current one. For fixed-point iteration, \[ e_{n+1}=F(x_n)-F(r), \] so the Mean Value Theorem gives \[ e_{n+1}=F'(\xi_n)e_n \] for some \(\xi_n\) between \(x_n\) and \(r\). That one line is the reason the derivative controls local convergence.

For bisection the update does not arise from differentiation. Instead, the uncertainty interval is halved each step, so the guaranteed midpoint error is halved as well. For Newton, Taylor expansion near \(r\) shows that the first-order error cancels, leaving a leading quadratic term. For secant, replacing the derivative by a difference quotient produces a more complicated recurrence and golden-ratio order. For elimination, one tracks how rounding error introduced in earlier stages is fed through later multipliers.

Behavior to recognize numerically

If \(|e_{n+1}|\approx c|e_n|\) with \(0<c<1\), expect linear convergence. If \(|e_{n+1}|\approx c|e_n|^2\), expect quadratic convergence. If the sign changes while the size decreases, the method is converging with oscillation, which often corresponds to a negative derivative at the fixed point. If the ratio \(|e_{n+1}|/|e_n|\) stays near 1, the method is converging very slowly or stalling. If the iterates move away from the target, the method is outside its basin of attraction or the local derivative conditions are not favorable.

Later in an actual computation, finite precision may overwhelm the exact-arithmetic theory. A method can be theoretically convergent and still stop improving because roundoff, cancellation, or poor conditioning has become dominant. That is why a successful analysis usually separates exact-arithmetic convergence from finite-precision effects.

Questions and Answers

Why is a small residual not automatically a small error?

Because \(r=Ae\). Recovering \(e\) from \(r\) requires multiplying by \(A^{-1}\), so a large \(\|A^{-1}\|\) can turn a small residual into a much larger solution error. That is exactly why the condition number appears in the residual-error inequality.

Why does Newton's method become so fast near a good root?

Because for a simple root the Newton correction cancels the first-order term in the Taylor expansion of \(f\) near the root. The leading remaining term is quadratic in the current error.

Why do interpolation error formulas contain derivatives of order \(n+1\)?

Because a degree-\(n\) polynomial already matches the lower-degree behavior of the function at the interpolation nodes. The remainder measures what is left after matching all polynomial information through degree \(n\), and that is why the first derivative not already absorbed into the interpolant is the \((n+1)\)st derivative.

Why do distinct nodes matter so much in interpolation?

Distinct nodes make the interpolation conditions independent. If nodes repeat without derivative data, the conditions collapse. Repeated nodes only make mathematical sense once derivative information is introduced, which is exactly what Hermite interpolation does.

Why do theorem hypotheses matter so much in numerical analysis?

Because the formula for a method can be algebraically meaningful even when the theorem guaranteeing good behavior does not apply. Numerical analysis is full of methods that are excellent under the right hypotheses and unreliable outside them. Checking the hypotheses is therefore part of using the method, not an optional extra.

Spline Interpolation

The new interpolation material shifts from one global polynomial to a piecewise-defined object. Given data \((x_j,y_j)\), we now seek a function \(S\) that interpolates the data, but is allowed to use a different polynomial on each subinterval. This is often better behaved than one high-degree polynomial because it respects local data while still preserving smoothness across the knots.

Basic definition and vocabulary

A spline of order \(k\) is a function that is a polynomial of degree at most \(k\) on each interval \([t_j,t_{j+1})\). The points \(t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_n\) are called knots. Standard names are: order \(0\) gives constant splines, order \(1\) gives linear splines, and order \(3\) gives cubic splines. In the interpolation setting one imposes \[ S(t_j)=f(t_j), \qquad j=0,1,\dots,n. \]

The notes explicitly avoid quadratic splines in this unit because one cannot impose the desired smoothness condition on \(S'\) at the knots in the same robust way that works for cubics. Cubic splines are the sweet spot: they are flexible enough to fit the data and smooth enough to look visually natural.

Natural cubic spline conditions

A natural cubic spline is a cubic spline satisfying the interpolation conditions together with the endpoint boundary conditions \[ S''(t_0)=0, \qquad S''(t_n)=0. \] These are called the natural boundary conditions. They remove the extra degrees of freedom left over after requiring piecewise cubic behavior and continuity of \(S\), \(S'\), and \(S''\) at interior knots.

The structural idea is to track the knot values of the second derivative. Write \[ z_j = S''(t_j), \qquad h_j=t_{j+1}-t_j. \] Once the vector \(z=(z_0,\dots,z_n)^T\) is known, each cubic piece \(S_j\) on \([t_j,t_{j+1}]\) is determined.

Closed form on each subinterval

For the interpolating cubic spline, the notes give the formula \[ S_j(x)= \frac{z_{j+1}}{6h_j}(x-t_j)^3 + \frac{z_j}{6h_j}(t_{j+1}-x)^3 + \left(\frac{y_{j+1}}{h_j}-\frac{h_j}{6}z_{j+1}\right)(x-t_j) + \left(\frac{y_j}{h_j}-\frac{h_j}{6}z_j\right)(t_{j+1}-x) \] for \(x\in[t_j,t_{j+1}]\). This formula is worth knowing because it separates the geometry of the knot spacing, the data values, and the second-derivative data. Once \(z_j\) and \(z_{j+1}\) are known, the cubic piece is completely explicit.

The same section also gives a nested form that is more convenient for implementation: \[ S_j(x)= y_j + (x-t_j)\bigl(C_j + (x-t_j)(B_j + (x-t_j)A_j)\bigr), \] where \[ A_j=\frac{z_{j+1}-z_j}{6h_j}, \qquad B_j=\frac{z_j}{2}, \] and \[ C_j=-\frac{h_j}{6}z_{j+1}-\frac{h_j}{3}z_j + \frac{y_{j+1}-y_j}{h_j}. \] The nested form matters computationally because it mirrors Horner-style evaluation and reduces unnecessary arithmetic.

The tridiagonal system

The unknowns \(z_0,\dots,z_n\) come from a tridiagonal linear system. Define \[ b_j = \frac{6}{h_j}(y_{j+1}-y_j), \qquad u_j=2(h_j+h_{j-1}), \qquad v_j=b_j-b_{j-1}. \] Then the natural spline system has the form \[ Az=v, \] where the first and last rows encode \(z_0=0\) and \(z_n=0\), and each interior row has the tridiagonal pattern \[ h_{j-1}z_{j-1}+u_j z_j + h_j z_{j+1}=v_j. \] This is one of the key computational reasons splines are practical: the matrix is sparse and tridiagonal, so solving for the spline coefficients is much cheaper than solving a dense system of the same size.

The notes also give a forward-elimination/back-substitution algorithm for this system. Conceptually, it is the same idea as the tridiagonal solver from the linear systems unit. That connection is worth keeping in mind: spline construction is not a completely separate topic. It is one more place where the linear algebra algorithms reappear.

Why splines are "visually pleasing"

Theoretical justification appears in the minimization theorem. If \(f\in C^2[a,b]\) and \(S\) is the natural cubic spline interpolating \(f\) at the knots, then \[ \int_a^b (S''(x))^2\,dx \le \int_a^b (f''(x))^2\,dx. \] In words, among all sufficiently smooth interpolants through the same knots, the natural cubic spline minimizes the bending energy measured by the square of the second derivative. This is why it tends to oscillate less than a high-degree global polynomial and why it is often described as the smoothest or most visually natural interpolant.

Endpoint variants and homework extensions

Natural boundary conditions are not the only choice. Homework 7 also studies endpoint derivative conditions such as \[ s'(0)=0, \qquad s'(n)=0. \] These modify the first and last equations while keeping the same tridiagonal interior structure. In the equally spaced case \(t_i=i\), the resulting coefficient matrix has diagonal entries \(2,4,\dots,4,2\) and adjacent off-diagonal entries \(1\). The homework solution emphasizes that this matrix is strictly diagonally dominant, hence invertible, so the spline is unique.

A good study habit here is to separate three layers. First identify which endpoint conditions are being imposed. Second write the corresponding linear system for the \(z_j\). Third use invertibility of the coefficient matrix to justify uniqueness. Most spline existence-and-uniqueness exercises follow exactly that pattern.

Numerical Differentiation

Numerical differentiation asks the opposite question from numerical integration. Instead of replacing a function by area data, we replace a derivative by values of the function itself. Because differentiation amplifies small perturbations, this topic is especially sensitive to truncation and roundoff, and the error constants matter.

Forward, backward, and central schemes

The three first-derivative formulas introduced in section 7.1 are \[ \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \quad \text{(forward difference)}, \] \[ \frac{f(x)-f(x-h)}{h} \quad \text{(backward difference)}, \] and \[ \frac{f(x+h)-f(x-h)}{2h} \quad \text{(central difference)}. \] All three come from Taylor expansion, but they do not have the same accuracy. The first two are one-sided and first-order. The central formula is symmetric and therefore cancels the first-order term, which is why it is more accurate.

Error terms and orders

If \(f\in C^1\) and \(f''\) exists, then the forward-difference error has the form \[ f'(x)-\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = -\frac{h}{2}f''(\omega), \] for some \(\omega\) between \(x\) and \(x+h\). So the forward scheme has truncation error \(O(h)\). The same order holds for the backward scheme.

If \(f\in C^3\), then the central-difference error is \[ f'(x)-\frac{f(x+h)-f(x-h)}{2h}= -\frac{h^2}{6}f^{(3)}(\omega), \] for some \(\omega\in(x-h,x+h)\). Thus the central scheme has error \(O(h^2)\). This higher order is one of the most important comparisons in the whole section.

Section 7.1 also gives the standard second-derivative approximation \[ \frac{f(x+h)-2f(x)+f(x-h)}{h^2}, \] together with the error formula \[ f''(x)-\frac{f(x+h)-2f(x)+f(x-h)}{h^2}= -\frac{h^2}{12}f^{(4)}(\omega), \] which again is \(O(h^2)\).

Truncation error

The notes define the truncation error of a difference scheme as the exact derivative minus the finite-difference approximation. That language matters because it isolates the error caused by replacing the derivative formula itself. It is distinct from roundoff error, which comes from finite precision after the scheme has been chosen.

In practice, smaller \(h\) does not automatically mean better answers forever. Truncation error shrinks with \(h\), but subtractive cancellation in \(f(x+h)-f(x)\) can cause roundoff to grow. A full computational discussion therefore balances truncation and roundoff, even when the theorem in class only proves a truncation bound.

Differentiating the interpolation formula

Section 7.1 connects finite differences to interpolation directly. If \[ f(x)=\sum_{j=0}^n f(x_j)\omega_j(x) + \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\varepsilon)}{(n+1)!}\prod_{k=0}^n (x-x_k), \] then differentiating at a node \(x_\vartheta\) gives \[ f'(x_\vartheta)=\sum_{j=0}^n f(x_j)\omega_j'(x_\vartheta)+ \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\varepsilon)}{(n+1)!}\prod_{k\ne \vartheta}(x_\vartheta-x_k). \] This viewpoint explains why difference formulas are really derivative formulas for interpolation polynomials plus an explicit remainder.

A related corollary says that when the remainder vanishes, the derivative of the interpolating polynomial at a node is exactly the weighted sum \[ p'(x_\vartheta)=\sum_{j=0}^n f(x_j)\omega_j'(x_\vartheta). \] This is a very useful conceptual bridge: numerical differentiation formulas are not arbitrary memorized quotients, but special cases of differentiating an interpolant.

Richardson extrapolation

One of the most important acceleration ideas in the section is Richardson extrapolation. Suppose an approximation \(\omega(h)\) satisfies \[ f'(x)=\omega(h)+O(h^2). \] Then the notes prove \[ f'(x)=\frac{4}{3}\omega(h/2)-\frac{1}{3}\omega(h)+O(h^4). \] The idea is to combine two second-order approximations so that the leading \(h^2\) term cancels.

The recursive table is written as \[ D(n,0)=\omega(h/2^n), \] and \[ D(n,k)=\frac{4^k D(n,k-1)-D(n-1,k-1)}{4^k-1}. \] The last diagonal entry is the most highly extrapolated value. The notes state that if enough derivatives exist, then each extra extrapolation level raises the asymptotic order. This is why Richardson is so useful: it upgrades a basic method without having to derive an entirely new stencil from scratch.

Numerical Integration

Numerical integration replaces an integral by a weighted sum of function values. In section 7.2, the main pattern is the same one seen in interpolation: derive a formula by integrating an interpolation polynomial, then compare the exact integral of \(f\) to the exact integral of that interpolant.

Trapezoid rule

On a single interval \([a,b]\), the trapezoid rule is \[ T(f)=\frac{b-a}{2}\bigl(f(a)+f(b)\bigr). \] Geometrically, it replaces the graph of \(f\) by the secant line through the endpoints and integrates the resulting trapezoid.

If \(f\in C^2\), the error formula is \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx - \frac{b-a}{2}\bigl(f(a)+f(b)\bigr) = -\frac{1}{12}(b-a)^3 f''(\omega) \] for some \(\omega\in(a,b)\). So the one-panel trapezoid rule is exact for polynomials of degree at most \(1\), but not exact for all quadratics.

The geometry also gives a quick qualitative test. If the graph is concave up, the secant line lies above the curve and the trapezoid rule overestimates the area. If the graph is concave down, the rule underestimates. This is not a substitute for the error theorem, but it is a good sign check.

Simpson's rule

Simpson's rule uses three equally spaced nodes and integrates the quadratic interpolant: \[ S(f)=\frac{b-a}{6}\left(f(a)+4f\!\left(\frac{a+b}{2}\right)+f(b)\right). \] Because it comes from quadratic interpolation, it is more accurate than the trapezoid rule on smooth data and is exact on a much larger polynomial class.

If \(f\in C^4\), the notes give \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx - \frac{b-a}{6}\left(f(a)+4f\!\left(\frac{a+b}{2}\right)+f(b)\right) = -\frac{1}{90}\left(\frac{b-a}{2}\right)^5 f^{(4)}(\omega) \] for some \(\omega\in(a,b)\). Simpson's rule is exact for all polynomials of degree at most \(3\), but not exact for every quartic.

Mean Value Theorem for integrals

The proof strategy in section 7.2 repeatedly uses the Mean Value Theorem for integrals: if \(u\) and \(v\) are continuous and \(v\ge 0\) on \([a,b]\), then there exists \(\omega\in[a,b]\) such that \[ \int_a^b u(x)v(x)\,dx = u(\omega)\int_a^b v(x)\,dx. \] This is how the error integrals collapse to a clean derivative evaluated at some intermediate point.

Composite formulas

On a uniform partition \(x_j=a+jh\) with \(h=(b-a)/n\), the composite trapezoid rule is \[ T_n=h\left(\frac12 f(x_0)+f(x_1)+\cdots+f(x_{n-1})+\frac12 f(x_n)\right), \] and the composite Simpson rule, for even \(n\), is \[ S_n=\frac{h}{3}\Bigl(f(x_0)+4f(x_1)+2f(x_2)+\cdots+4f(x_{n-1})+f(x_n)\Bigr). \] The alternating \(4,2,4,2,\dots,4\) pattern in Simpson's rule is worth memorizing carefully, because small bookkeeping mistakes there are extremely common.

The composite error theorems are \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx - T_n = -\frac{1}{12}(b-a)h^2 f''(\omega)=O(h^2) \] when \(f\in C^2\), and \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx - S_n = -\frac{1}{180}(b-a)h^4 f^{(4)}(\omega)=O(h^4) \] when \(f\in C^4\) and \(n\) is even. These are some of the cleanest order statements in the course. Composite Simpson improves the order from \(2\) to \(4\), provided the function is smooth enough.

What exactness really means

A quadrature rule is exact on a class of functions if it reproduces the true integral of every function in that class. The trapezoid rule is exact on linear polynomials because it integrates the line joining the endpoints, which is the function itself when \(f\) is linear. Simpson's rule is exact up through cubics even though it is derived from a quadratic interpolant. That extra exactness is one of the reasons Simpson's rule is so effective.

Gaussian Quadrature

Gaussian quadrature asks a sharper question than the composite rules: if only a small number of function values may be used, where should the nodes be placed and what weights should be chosen so that the degree of exactness is as large as possible? Section 7.3 answers this on \([-1,1]\) by choosing the nodes very carefully instead of spacing them uniformly.

General form and the cardinal functions

One seeks an approximation of the form \[ \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx \sum_{j=0}^n A_j f(x_j). \] If the nodes \(x_0,\dots,x_n\) are fixed, the weights are given by integrating the Lagrange cardinal functions: \[ A_j=\int_{-1}^{1} \omega_j(x)\,dx, \] where \[ \omega_j(x)=\prod_{k\ne j}\frac{x-x_k}{x_j-x_k}. \] For every polynomial \(q\) of degree at most \(n\), this gives the exact identity \[ \int_{-1}^{1} q(x)\,dx = \sum_{j=0}^n A_j q(x_j). \]

The real gain appears when the nodes are not arbitrary. If they are chosen as the roots of a polynomial \(q\) of degree \(n+1\) that is orthogonal to \(1,x,\dots,x^n\) on \([-1,1]\), then the resulting quadrature becomes exact for every polynomial of degree at most \(2n+1\). This is the Gaussian quadrature theorem.

Why the degree of exactness jumps to \(2n+1\)

The proof idea is elegant. If \(p\) has degree at most \(2n+1\), divide by the orthogonal polynomial \(q\) to write \[ p=qm+r, \] where both \(m\) and \(r\) have degree at most \(n\). Orthogonality makes the integral of \(qm\) disappear, so \[ \int_{-1}^{1} p(x)\,dx = \int_{-1}^{1} r(x)\,dx. \] Since \(r\) has degree at most \(n\), the Lagrange-weight formula integrates it exactly. At each Gaussian node \(x_j\), the factor \(q(x_j)=0\), so \(p(x_j)=r(x_j)\). That is why the final weighted sum recovers the exact integral of \(p\).

Legendre polynomials and Gaussian nodes

In this section the relevant orthogonal polynomials are the Legendre polynomials. They satisfy \[ q_0(x)=1, \qquad q_1(x)=x, \qquad q_2(x)=\frac32 x^2-\frac12, \] and the recurrence \[ q_n(x)=\frac{2n-1}{n}xq_{n-1}(x)-\frac{n-1}{n}q_{n-2}(x). \] The notes state that the roots of \(q_n\) are simple, lie in \((-1,1)\), and generate the Gaussian nodes of order \(n\).

The first useful cases are worth memorizing: \[ n=1: \quad x_0=-\frac{1}{\sqrt 3}, \; x_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt 3}, \qquad A_0=A_1=1, \] and \[ n=2: \quad x_0=-\sqrt{\frac35}, \; x_1=0, \; x_2=\sqrt{\frac35}, \qquad A_0=A_2=\frac59, \; A_1=\frac89. \] These formulas appear constantly in worked examples.

Positivity and normalization of the weights

The Gaussian coefficients are all positive and satisfy \[ \sum_{j=0}^n A_j = \int_{-1}^{1} 1\,dx = 2. \] This is a small statement with a lot of practical value. Positivity means the rule behaves like a genuine weighted average rather than a cancellation-prone oscillatory formula, and the normalization check gives an immediate way to test whether a computed set of weights is plausible.

Changing intervals

Gaussian quadrature is derived on \([-1,1]\), but the notes explain how to move to a general interval \([a,b]\) by the change of variables \[ x=\frac{b+a+t(b-a)}{2}. \] Then \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \frac{b-a}{2}\int_{-1}^{1} f\!\left(\frac{b+a+t(b-a)}{2}\right)dt. \] So in practice one transforms the integrand, evaluates it at the Gaussian nodes \(t=x_j\), multiplies by the weights, and finally multiplies by \((b-a)/2\).

Convergence and comparison to Simpson

Using the Weierstrass approximation theorem, the notes prove that Gaussian quadrature converges for every continuous function on \([-1,1]\): as the order increases, \[ \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx - \sum_{j=0}^n A_j f(x_j) \to 0. \] This is a very different style of result from the composite trapezoid and composite Simpson theorems. For those methods, the error is expressed as \(O(h^2)\) or \(O(h^4)\) in terms of a mesh size \(h\). For Gaussian quadrature, there is no equally simple uniform-spacing parameter, so the notes explicitly warn that we do not get an easy \(O(h^k)\)-style expression.

The section also warns that Gaussian quadrature does not mix naturally with interpolation on uniformly spaced nodes. The strength of the method comes from choosing special nodes adapted to orthogonality, not from reusing arbitrary data that happen to be already sampled at equally spaced points.

Romberg Integration

Romberg integration improves the composite trapezoid rule by combining two ideas: first, reuse old trapezoid data when the mesh is refined, and second, apply Richardson extrapolation to cancel the leading error term. The goal is to get a much more accurate approximation of \[ I=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \] without repeatedly rebuilding everything from scratch.

Recursive trapezoid rule

Let \[ R(n,0)=T_{2^n}, \] where \(T_{2^n}\) is the composite trapezoid-rule approximation with \(2^n\) subintervals. If \[ h_n=\frac{b-a}{2^n}, \] then the recursive formula from the notes is \[ R(0,0)=\frac{b-a}{2}\bigl(f(a)+f(b)\bigr), \] and for \(n\ge 1\), \[ R(n,0)=\frac12 R(n-1,0)+h_n\sum_{j=1}^{2^{n-1}} f\bigl(a+(2j-1)h_n\bigr). \] This formula is important because it shows exactly which new function values must be added when the mesh is halved: only the odd-indexed midpoint samples are new.

On \([0,1]\), the first few steps make the pattern concrete: \[ T_2=\frac12 T_1+\frac12 f\!\left(\frac12\right), \] \[ T_4=\frac12 T_2+\frac14\left(f\!\left(\frac14\right)+f\!\left(\frac34\right)\right), \] \[ T_8=\frac12 T_4+\frac18\left(f\!\left(\frac18\right)+f\!\left(\frac38\right)+f\!\left(\frac58\right)+f\!\left(\frac78\right)\right). \] The algorithm therefore refines the mesh efficiently instead of restarting at every level.

Richardson extrapolation inside the Romberg table

Once the first column \(R(n,0)\) has been computed, the higher columns are defined by \[ R(n,m)=R(n,m-1)+\frac{R(n,m-1)-R(n-1,m-1)}{4^m-1}, \qquad 1\le m\le n. \] The denominator \(4^m-1\) appears because the composite trapezoid rule has an error expansion in even powers of \(h\). Each extrapolation step cancels one more leading term in that expansion, so the diagonal entries of the Romberg table are progressively more accurate than the raw trapezoid approximations.

The conceptual pattern is the same one used earlier in differentiation: if two approximations have the same leading error term but at different step sizes, then an appropriate linear combination can remove that leading term. Romberg integration simply packages that cancellation into a triangular table of values.

How to use Romberg in practice

A good way to work a Romberg problem by hand is to proceed in columns. First compute \(R(0,0),R(1,0),\dots,R(M,0)\) using the recursive trapezoid rule. Then compute \(R(1,1)\), then \(R(2,1)\) and \(R(2,2)\), and continue until the desired diagonal value is reached. The entry \(R(n,n)\) is usually the best approximation available at depth \(n\), because it has undergone the most extrapolation.

Adaptive Quadrature

Uniform meshes are simple, but they can waste evaluations on regions where the integrand is already easy and undersample regions where the integrand bends sharply. Adaptive quadrature fixes this by deciding where to refine based on an error estimate. In the lecture notes, the method is built from Simpson's rule rather than the trapezoid rule.

Basic setup with Simpson's rule

On an interval \([u,v]\), Simpson's rule gives an approximation \[ S(u,v)\approx \int_u^v f(x)\,dx. \] If \(w=(u+v)/2\), then applying Simpson's rule separately on the two half-intervals gives \[ S(u,w)+S(w,v). \] The single-panel approximation \(S(u,v)\) and the two-panel approximation \(S(u,w)+S(w,v)\) have related error terms, and that relation can be exploited with Richardson extrapolation.

Under the usual assumption that the fourth derivative is approximately constant across the interval, the notes derive the corrected approximation \[ \int_u^v f(x)\,dx \approx S(u,w)+S(w,v)+\frac{1}{15}\Bigl(S(u,w)+S(w,v)-S(u,v)\Bigr). \] The term \[ \frac{1}{15}\Bigl(S(u,w)+S(w,v)-S(u,v)\Bigr) \] is used as an estimate of the Simpson error on \([u,v]\). This is the adaptive analogue of the Romberg correction term.

Acceptance test and refinement logic

The adaptive idea is simple: compute \(S(u,v)\), compute the two half-interval values \(S(u,w)\) and \(S(w,v)\), and compare them. If \[ \left|S(u,w)+S(w,v)-S(u,v)\right| \] is sufficiently small relative to the prescribed tolerance, accept the corrected value \[ S(u,w)+S(w,v)+\frac{1}{15}\Bigl(S(u,w)+S(w,v)-S(u,v)\Bigr). \] Otherwise, subdivide again and repeat the same procedure on \([u,w]\) and \([w,v]\) separately.

This explains why adaptive quadrature places more nodes where the function is difficult. Smooth parts of the interval quickly pass the acceptance test, while highly curved or rapidly changing parts are subdivided further. The method is therefore not just a formula; it is a refinement strategy driven by a local error estimate.

Implementation viewpoint

The notes give both an iterative stack-based description and a recursive description. In either version, one saves function values that have already been computed so they are not evaluated again unnecessarily. This bookkeeping is part of what makes the method efficient. The real computational advantage of adaptive quadrature is not merely the correction term; it is the combination of local error control and data reuse.

Initial-Value Problems for Ordinary Differential Equations

The new model problem in the ODE unit is the initial-value problem \[ x'(t)=f(t,x), \qquad x(t_0)=x_0. \] Here the unknown is not a number but a function. The numerical question is to approximate the value of the solution at later times, such as \(x(t_0+h)\), \(x(t_0+2h)\), and so on.

Slope fields

A slope field for \[ x'(t)=f(t,x) \] is obtained by plotting, at each point \((t,x)\), a short line segment with slope \(f(t,x)\). This gives a geometric picture of the differential equation before one solves it exactly. A solution curve must be tangent to the local line segments, so the slope field is the geometric backdrop for Euler's method and the midpoint method. In the lecture example \[ x'(t)=tx+t^3, \qquad x(0)=1, \] the slope field shows immediately how different initial values lead to different trajectories even though the same differential equation is being followed.

Exact solution example

The notes begin the section by solving \[ x'(t)=tx+t^3, \qquad x(0)=1, \] exactly with an integrating factor. Writing the equation as \[ x'(t)-tx=t^3, \] an integrating factor is \[ \mu(t)=e^{-t^2/2}. \] After multiplying through and integrating, the specific solution is \[ x(t)=-t^2-2+3e^{t^2/2}. \] This exact answer is useful because it lets one compare later numerical approximations against a known solution.

Existence and uniqueness

The section also introduces the foundational theorems that justify numerical methods. If \(f\) is continuous on a rectangle \[ A=\{(t,x): |t-t_0|\le \omega,\ |x-x_0|\le \varepsilon\}, \] then there exists at least one solution of the initial-value problem on a time interval of length controlled by the bound \[ M=\max_{(t,x)\in A}|f(t,x)|. \] More precisely, the solution exists for \[ |t-t_0|\le \min\!\left(\omega,\frac{\varepsilon}{M}\right). \]

If, in addition, \(f_x\) is continuous on the same rectangle, then the solution is unique on that interval. A more general uniqueness condition used in the notes is the Lipschitz condition in the \(x\)-variable: if there exists \(L\ge 0\) such that \[ |f(t,x_1)-f(t,x_2)|\le L|x_1-x_2| \] for all relevant \(t,x_1,x_2\), then the initial-value problem has a unique solution. This is the theorem-level reason one is allowed to speak of the solution curve passing through \((t_0,x_0)\).

Euler's Method, Richardson Improvement, and the Midpoint Method

Numerical ODE methods begin by approximating the exact identity \[ x(t+h)=x(t)+\int_t^{t+h} f(s,x(s))\,ds. \] If one replaces the integral by a left-endpoint rule, then the first approximation is \[ x(t+h)\approx x(t)+h f(t,x(t)). \] This is the same conceptual move as in numerical integration: replace an exact integral by a simple quadrature formula.

Forward Euler method

The Forward Euler Method is defined by \[ x_{k+1}=x_k+h f(t_k,x_k), \qquad t_k=t_0+kh. \] In words, one follows the slope field from the current point using the slope at the left endpoint of the time step. The method is explicit and easy to compute, but it uses only one slope evaluation per step and so it is only a first-order method.

The notes emphasize the geometry: starting from \((t_k,x_k)\), the method moves horizontally by \(h\) and vertically by \(h f(t_k,x_k)\). This makes Euler's method a direct numerical path-tracing procedure through the slope field.

Richardson improvement for a first-order method

If one Euler step of length \(h\) is compared to two Euler steps of length \(h/2\), then the two approximations have the same leading first-order error term. Richardson's idea is to combine them so that this leading term cancels. The notes use this observation to motivate a better slope choice without having to compute analytic derivatives of \(f\).

Modified Euler method / midpoint formula

The resulting method is the Modified Euler Method, also called the Midpoint Formula: \[ x_{k+1}=x_k+h f\!\left(t_k+\frac{h}{2},x_k+\frac{h}{2}f(t_k,x_k)\right). \] It works in two stages. First estimate the midpoint value by \[ x_{k+1/2}=x_k+\frac{h}{2}f(t_k,x_k). \] Then evaluate the slope at that midpoint and use it for the full step. This uses two slope evaluations per step, but it tracks the local curvature of the solution much better than Forward Euler.

The practical lesson is that Forward Euler uses the slope at the beginning of the interval, while the midpoint method uses a slope sampled closer to the center of the interval. That is why the midpoint method usually produces a noticeably better approximation on the same mesh.

Taylor-Series Methods for ODEs

Taylor-series methods approximate the solution of an initial-value problem by expanding the exact solution in powers of \(h\). If \(x\) is smooth and satisfies \[ x'(t)=f(t,x), \] then the \(n\)th-order Taylor method is based on \[ x(t+h)=x(t)+h x'(t)+\frac{h^2}{2}x''(t)+\cdots+\frac{h^n}{n!}x^{(n)}(t). \] Since \(x'(t)=f(t,x)\), the method can be written in terms of \(f\) and its total derivatives along the solution curve: \[ x(t+h)=x(t)+h f(t,x)+\frac{h^2}{2}\frac{d}{dt}f(t,x)+\cdots+\frac{h^n}{n!}\frac{d^{n-1}}{dt^{n-1}}f(t,x). \]

Why the derivatives must be computed carefully

A major warning from the notes is that one must not differentiate \(f(t,x)\) as if \(x\) were independent of \(t\). For example, if \[ x'(t)=tx+t^3, \] then \[ x''(t)=\frac{d}{dt}(tx+t^3)=x+t x'(t)+3t^2, \] not merely \(x+3t^2\). The extra term appears because \(x\) itself depends on \(t\). This is a chain-rule issue, not a minor notation point.

Local truncation error

If the exact solution value \(x(t_0+h)\) is approximated by \(x_1\), then the local truncation error is \[ x(t_0+h)-x_1. \] The theorem in the notes states that an \(n\)th-order Taylor approximation has local truncation error of size \[ O(h^{n+1}). \] This is the direct Taylor-remainder statement and is one of the cleanest error results in the ODE unit.

In particular, the first-order Taylor approximation \[ x(t+h)\approx x(t)+h f(t,x) \] is exactly Euler's method, so its local truncation error is \(O(h^2)\). Adding the second derivative term produces a second-order Taylor method with local truncation error \(O(h^3)\).

Taylor's theorem in two variables

The section also states Taylor's theorem for a smooth function \(f(x,y)\) of two variables. For increments \((h,k)\), one expands \[ f(x+h,y+k) \] using powers of the differential operator \[ h\frac{\partial}{\partial x}+k\frac{\partial}{\partial y}. \] At second order, this produces terms involving \[ f_x,\quad f_y,\quad f_{xx},\quad f_{xy},\quad f_{yy}, \] together with an explicit remainder term. In the lecture, this two-variable expansion is used to compare the midpoint method against the second-order Taylor method and to explain why midpoint captures the same leading behavior without requiring analytic derivatives of \(f\).

This is the key tradeoff of Taylor methods: they offer a very transparent error theory and can be made arbitrarily high order in principle, but every increase in order requires more analytic derivative information. That drawback is exactly what motivates multistage methods that estimate higher-order behavior by sampling the slope field at several nearby points instead.

Homework Solution Extensions and Worked Study Patterns

The homework sets add a second layer to the notes. The lectures give general theorems and algorithms; the homework solutions show what those ideas look like when carried all the way through an actual argument or computation. These patterns are exactly the kind that tend to reappear on quizzes, midterms, and proof-based writeups.

Homework 6: pivoting, conditioning, and refinement

One of the clearest lessons from Homework 6 is that pivoting is not cosmetic. In the rounded elimination example, solving without pivoting produced an \(\ell_1\)-error of about \(0.0329\), while using partial pivoting reduced it to about \(0.0087\). The conceptual takeaway is simple: pivoting keeps multipliers smaller, and that usually controls the spread of roundoff through the elimination process.

The scaled partial pivoting problem reinforces the same point from a slightly different angle. There the correct pivot decisions are based on ratios of candidate pivots to row scales, not raw entry size alone. That distinction matters when one row is globally larger than another and raw comparison would be misleading.

Another important pattern is extracting determinants from \(PA=LU\). Once the factorization is known, \[ \det(A)=\frac{\det(L)\det(U)}{\det(P)}. \] Since \(L\) is unit lower triangular, \(\det(L)=1\), and for triangular matrices the determinant is just the product of diagonal entries. That makes determinant questions after elimination much faster than recomputing from scratch.

Homework 6 also includes an illuminating Vandermonde-style system with entries \(a_{ij}=(i-1)^{j-1}\). The exact solution is the coordinate vector \(e_2\), but MATLAB begins to struggle for large \(n\) because the system becomes extremely ill-conditioned. This is a textbook example of the difference between a problem having a perfectly simple exact answer and the corresponding numerical system still being hard to solve accurately.

The residual-correction problem gives the cleanest possible version of iterative refinement. Starting from an approximate solution \(\tilde x\), compute \(r=b-A\tilde x\). Then solve \(Ay=r\) and update \(\tilde x\leftarrow \tilde x+y\). In the worked 2-by-2 example, the corrected vector solves the system exactly. The lesson is that the residual is not just a diagnostic. It can be turned into a correction equation.

The tridiagonal-operation-count problem is also worth remembering. Solving the system by exploiting the tridiagonal structure takes \[ 3(n-1) \] additions or subtractions and \[ 5n-4 \] multiplications or divisions. This is much cheaper than pretending the matrix is dense. In numerical analysis, recognizing structure is often as important as executing the algorithm.

Two small norm-based examples from the same homework are also good exam patterns. First, the inequality \[ \max_{x\in\Omega}(f(x)+g(x)) \le \max_{x\in\Omega} f(x)+\max_{x\in\Omega} g(x) \] is proved by bounding each term pointwise and then maximizing. Second, the matrix \[ A=\begin{pmatrix}2^2 & 2^3 \\ 2^3 & 2^4\end{pmatrix} \] has \(\|A\|_\infty=47\), \(\|A^{-1}\|_\infty=47\), and therefore condition number \(2209\). A large condition number warns that small data errors can lead to large solution errors, so one should not expect uniformly tiny errors in finite precision.

The 2-norm problem for \[ A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix} \] is another helpful pattern. By parameterizing \(x\) on the unit circle or by using Lagrange multipliers, one finds \(\|A\|_2=4\). The message is that norm questions often hide an optimization problem, and sometimes a clever parameterization solves it faster than brute force algebra.

Homework 7: interpolation and spline practice

Homework 7 begins with concrete Newton-form exercises. One interpolant is written as \[ p(x)=2+x-3x(x-2)+4x(x-2)(x-3), \] and then converted into nested form. This is a good reminder that Newton form is not just for theory. It is computationally useful and easy to update when new nodes are added.

Another early exercise tests whether tabulated data come from a cubic polynomial. The right strategy is not to fit all six points at once immediately. Instead, interpolate four of them with a cubic, then check whether the remaining data values agree. That is a standard uniqueness-based pattern: once a degree bound is known, a small set of data can determine the whole answer.

The interpolation-error problems in Homework 7 are especially important. For \(\sin(x)\) on \([0,1]\), the basic estimate \[ |\sin(x)-p_n(x)|\le \frac{1}{(n+1)!} \] already shows that \(n\ge 10\) is sufficient for error below \(10^{-7}\). The homework also points out that this estimate is coarse because the product term can often be bounded more sharply. That is a good exam lesson: one should always distinguish a correct bound from a sharp bound.

The ODE-guided interpolation problem is a model example of how to combine analysis and approximation. For the linear interpolant on \([a,a+h]\), the error bound becomes \[ |e_1|\le \frac{h^2}{8}(a+h)y_1. \] For the quadratic interpolant on \([a,a+2h]\), the bound becomes \[ |e_2|\le \frac{\sqrt{3}h^3}{27}\bigl(y_1+(a+2h)|f'(a)|\bigr). \] The structure matters more than the exact constants: first write the remainder theorem, then bound the derivative term using the ODE, then optimize the node product term separately.

A very useful fact from the same homework is that if \(p\) is a polynomial of degree \(n\), then the divided difference \[ p[x_0,x_1,\dots,x_{n+1}] \] is zero. This is immediate from the mean value theorem for divided differences because \(p^{(n+1)}\equiv 0\). It is exactly the kind of short fact that often appears as a conceptual checkpoint on exams.

The Newton divided-difference tables in Homework 7 also show an important practical pattern: when a new node is added, one can often recycle the old table instead of starting from nothing. That reuse is one of the main computational advantages of Newton form over a full re-derivation in Lagrange form.

The Hermite interpolation problem is another strong template. From the data \[ f(0)=1, \quad f(1)=2, \quad f'(1)=-1, \quad f(3)=0, \quad f'(3)=0, \] the homework builds the Hermite polynomial and obtains \[ f(2)\approx \frac{11}{18}. \] If \(|f^{(5)}|\le 4\), the error estimate becomes \[ |f(2)-p(2)|\le \frac{1}{15}. \] The study point is that repeated nodes in the Hermite table count with multiplicity in the remainder formula.

Another elegant exercise asks for a polynomial \(p\) of degree at most \(n\) such that \(p(a)=f(a)\) and \(p'(j)=f'(j)\) for \(j=1,\dots,n\). The solution is to interpolate the derivative data first with a degree-\((n-1)\) polynomial \(q\), then integrate: \[ p(x)=f(a)+\int_a^x q(t)\,dt. \] This is a beautiful example of reducing one interpolation problem to another by differentiation and integration.

The spline questions at the end of Homework 7 are also instructive because they force you to translate smoothness conditions into algebraic constraints. For instance, a proposed piecewise cubic may fail to be a natural spline because continuity of \(s\), \(s'\), and the endpoint conditions \(s''(-1)=s''(1)=0\) cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. By contrast, a different piecewise definition can be made into a cubic spline once continuity conditions and the given third-derivative condition are solved together.

The final homework problem proves uniqueness of a cubic spline with endpoint derivative conditions \(s'(0)=s'(n)=0\) by setting up the linear system for the second-derivative data and observing diagonal dominance. That is exactly the kind of cross-topic argument this course likes: an interpolation object is proved unique by a linear algebra theorem.
